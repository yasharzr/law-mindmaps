Associate Judge vs. Judge: Associate Judges (formerly Masters) hear motions and procedural matters; Judges hear trials and major motions. Associate Judge orders are appealable to a SCJ judge.

Trap: Divisional Court hears appeals from interlocutory orders and judicial review applications — not the Court of Appeal. Don't mix these up.
▶ r. 2.1 — Frivolous/Vexatious Proceedings Rule

Court may, on its own initiative, stay or dismiss a proceeding that appears on its face to be frivolous, vexatious, or an abuse of process.

Parties may also bring a motion under r. 2.1 to have a proceeding stayed or dismissed.

Threshold: High bar — only where the case is "manifestly and clearly" without merit. Does not replace r. 21 (motion before trial).
▶ W2: Professional Conduct & Ethics Rules of Prof. Conduct
▶ Lawyer Competence

Rules of Professional Conduct (Law Society of Ontario) — govern lawyer conduct in civil proceedings.

Competence (r. 3.1-2): Lawyer must apply relevant knowledge, skills and attributes to representation. Includes knowing procedure, knowing the facts, and applying diligence.

Candour to tribunal (r. 5.1-2): Lawyer must not knowingly deceive the court. Must disclose adverse controlling authority. Cannot mislead through acts or omissions.

Key obligations in civil proceedings:
• Serve client resolutely but within the law
• Not make frivolous arguments or claims
• Not take positions that will delay or increase costs unnecessarily (links to r. 1.04)
• Advise client of ADR options (r. 24.1 mandatory mediation)
▶ Client Relationships & Confidentiality

Solicitor-client privilege: Communications for purpose of obtaining legal advice are privileged. Cannot be disclosed in discovery or at trial without client waiver.

Litigation privilege: Documents prepared for dominant purpose of litigation are protected from disclosure. Distinct from solicitor-client privilege — ends when litigation ends.

Without prejudice communications: Settlement discussions protected from use as admissions. Protects candour in negotiation.

Trap: Litigation privilege is not absolute — once litigation ends, privilege may be lost. Solicitor-client privilege is permanent unless waived.
▶ W3: Jurisdiction r. 3
▶ Territorial Jurisdiction r. 3.02

Ontario courts may assume jurisdiction over defendants outside Ontario where there is a real and substantial connection to Ontario.

Factors establishing connection (Morguard / Club Resorts principles):

  • Defendant domiciled or resident in Ontario
  • Contract made or to be performed in Ontario
  • Tort committed in Ontario
  • Real property situated in Ontario
  • Defendant carried on business in Ontario

r. 3.02: Service outside Ontario permitted where one of the enumerated connections exists (formerly ex juris service).

FIRAC — Territorial Jurisdiction Issue

F: Defendant located in [X province/country]; cause of action arose in [Y]
I: Does Ontario SCJ have territorial jurisdiction over defendant?
R: Real & substantial connection test (Morguard); r. 3.02 enumerated bases for service ex juris
A: Identify which connecting factor(s) apply — e.g., tort in Ontario, contract to be performed in Ontario
C: If connection exists: jurisdiction established; if not: forum may decline or lack jurisdiction
▶ Subject Matter Jurisdiction

SCJ has unlimited civil jurisdiction (CJA s. 11). No minimum/maximum claim amount.

Small Claims Court: Claims up to $35,000 (as of 2020). Simplified procedure.

Consent jurisdiction: Parties may consent to jurisdiction they would otherwise lack (e.g., forum selection clauses).

Forum non conveniens: Even if jurisdiction exists, court may decline to exercise it if another forum is clearly more appropriate. Factors: location of parties, witnesses, evidence, applicable law.

Trap: Federal matters (e.g., patents, bankruptcy, immigration) go to Federal Court, not SCJ. Subject matter jurisdiction is distinct from territorial jurisdiction.
▶ Limitation Periods Limitations Act 2002

Basic limitation period: 2 years from the date the claim was discovered (s. 4, Limitations Act 2002).

Ultimate limitation period: 15 years from the date the act/omission occurred (s. 15) — regardless of discovery.

Discoverability: Claim discovered when plaintiff knew or ought to have known: (1) injury occurred; (2) caused by defendant; (3) sufficiently serious to warrant proceeding (s. 5).

Trap: Limitation periods are a defence — the defendant must plead it. Court cannot raise it on its own. Always check: when did cause of action arise? When was it discovered? Has 2 years elapsed?

FIRAC — Limitation Period

F: Plaintiff suffered injury on [date]; commenced action on [date]
I: Is the claim statute-barred under the Limitations Act 2002?
R: s. 4: 2-year basic period from discovery; s. 5: discoverability; s. 15: 15-year ultimate cap
A: When did plaintiff know/ought to have known? Calculate 2 years from that date.
C: If >2 years: claim prima facie statute-barred (subject to discoverability argument)
▶ W4: Pleadings r. 14, 18, 25
▶ Statement of Claim — r. 14

Originating process: An action begins with a Statement of Claim (r. 14.01). Application begins with a Notice of Application.

Contents required (r. 25.06):

  • Every material fact (not evidence, not legal conclusions)
  • Nature of claim — what relief is sought
  • Damages must be particularized (special damages specifically)
  • Parties correctly named and capacity stated

Issuance: Claim issued by court (stamp & seal); backsheet; court file number assigned.

Validity: Statement of Claim valid for 6 months for service within Ontario; 12 months for service outside Ontario (r. 14.08).

Material Facts Rule (Goma v Raghunandan): Must plead material facts — the "who, what, when, where, how." Legal conclusions are not enough. Evidence is not pleaded.

FIRAC — Defective Statement of Claim

F: Plaintiff files claim containing only legal conclusions / omits key facts
I: Is the Statement of Claim defective for failing to plead material facts?
R: r. 25.06: must plead material facts; r. 21.01(1)(b): court may strike pleading disclosing no reasonable cause of action
A: Identify what is missing (facts vs conclusions); can the defect be cured by amendment?
C: Court may strike pleading or order particulars; amendment permitted if no prejudice
▶ Statement of Defence — r. 18

Timing (r. 18.01): Defendant must deliver defence within 20 days of service of claim (if served in Ontario). Extended to 40 days if served outside Ontario but within Canada; 60 days if served outside Canada.

Deemed admissions (r. 25.07): Any allegation not specifically denied is deemed admitted. Defendant must either admit, deny, or plead no knowledge of each allegation.

Affirmative defences: Must be specifically pleaded — limitation period, contributory negligence, release, res judicata, etc.

Default judgment (r. 19): If defendant fails to deliver statement of defence, plaintiff may note defendant in default and seek default judgment.

Trap: Deemed admissions — if defence says "the defendant denies paragraphs 1 to 5" without specifying which facts are denied, everything else is admitted. Must be specific.
▶ Reply & Subsequent Pleadings — r. 25

Reply (r. 25.08): Plaintiff may deliver reply to any new matter raised in defence. Not mandatory. Must be delivered within 10 days of defence.

Close of pleadings: After defence (or reply, if any), pleadings are closed. Parties then move to discovery.

Amendments (r. 26): Pleadings may be amended with leave of court or consent of parties. Court grants leave unless amendment would cause prejudice not compensable by costs. Relation-back doctrine for amendments adding new parties.

Pleadings serve four functions: (1) define the issues; (2) give notice to the other side; (3) limit the evidence at trial; (4) form the basis for the judgment.
▶ Striking Pleadings — r. 21 Bayliss v Wren Lake

r. 21.01: A party may move before trial to have a pleading struck on the basis that it:

  • (1)(a) Discloses no reasonable cause of action or defence
  • (1)(b) Is scandalous, frivolous, or vexatious
  • (1)(c) May prejudice, embarrass or delay a fair trial
  • (1)(d) Is otherwise an abuse of process

Standard (Bayliss v Wren Lake Estates): Court assumes all pleaded facts are true. Question is whether it is "plain and obvious" that the claim cannot succeed. High threshold.

On a r. 21 motion: No evidence is filed. The court looks at the pleadings only (on their face).

Trap: r. 21 motion cannot be used to test credibility or weigh conflicting facts. That is for trial or summary judgment. If there is any arguable point of law, the pleading survives.

FIRAC — r. 21 Motion to Strike

F: Defendant moves to strike plaintiff's claim as disclosing no cause of action
I: Should the pleading be struck under r. 21.01?
R: "Plain and obvious" test; all pleaded facts assumed true; no evidence admitted
A: Does the pleading, if all facts accepted, disclose a known cause of action? Any arguable legal basis?
C: If plain and obvious it cannot succeed: struck (with or without leave to amend); if arguable: motion dismissed
▶ W5: Service r. 16
▶ Methods of Personal Service — r. 16.02

Crown (Ontario)Leave with a solicitor in Ministry of Attorney General

Originating process (Statement of Claim) must be served personally or by an accepted alternative method. Subsequent documents may be served by alternatives.

▶ Alternatives to Personal Service

Accepted alternatives (r. 16.03):

  • Acceptance of service by lawyer
  • Mail (with 5-day rule — deemed served 5 days after mailing)
  • Courier
  • Fax (with confirmation)
  • Email (with prior written consent or court order)

Deemed service: Where a document is served by mail or courier, it is deemed served on a later date (to account for transit time). Timing affects deadlines for responding.

Deemed service dates: Mail → 5 days after mailing; Courier → 2 days after dispatch; Fax → same day if sent before 5pm on a business day, else next business day.
▶ Service Outside Ontario & Substitutional Service Mullin v Lagace

Service outside Ontario (r. 17): Permitted where a connection to Ontario exists (same factors as territorial jurisdiction). Leave may be required depending on basis for service.

Substitutional service (r. 16.04): Where personal service is not practicable, court may order substitutional service (e.g., posting on door, leaving with adult at residence, publication in newspaper).

Mullin v Lagace: To obtain substitutional service order, must show (1) genuine attempts to serve personally; (2) personal service is not practicable; (3) proposed method will likely bring proceeding to defendant's attention.

Trap: Substitutional service is not routine — must make genuine attempts at personal service first. Court is reluctant to order it absent evidence of those attempts.

FIRAC — Substitutional Service

F: Plaintiff cannot locate defendant to serve Statement of Claim personally
I: Can plaintiff obtain an order for substitutional service?
R: r. 16.04; Mullin v Lagace three-part test
A: Evidence of attempts; impracticability; proposed method likely to come to defendant's attention
C: Order granted specifying method; failure to comply with order may result in proceeding being set aside
▶ W6: Motion Practice r. 37, 39
▶ Motions Generally — r. 37

Motion: An application to the court for an order in the course of a proceeding. Used to resolve procedural disputes without trial.

Notice of motion (r. 37.03): Must state the date, time, place, grounds for motion, and relief sought. Served on all parties.

r. 37.09: Court may dismiss a motion or grant an order if the responding party fails to appear.

r. 1.04 on motions: Court considers proportionality — motions should not be used to drive up costs. Costs consequences for unnecessary or unsuccessful motions.
▶ Interlocutory Injunctions Hoq v Doe

Three-part test (RJR-MacDonald / Hoq v Doe):

  • (1) Serious issue to be tried: Is there an arguable case (not frivolous or vexatious)?
  • (2) Irreparable harm: Will plaintiff suffer harm not compensable in damages if injunction refused?
  • (3) Balance of convenience: Does balance of harm favour granting or refusing the injunction?

Undertaking as to damages: Plaintiff must give an undertaking to compensate defendant for any loss if injunction is wrongly granted.

Trap: "Serious issue" is low threshold — not balance of probabilities. But if strong prima facie case established, court may go straight to balance of convenience.

FIRAC — Interlocutory Injunction

F: Plaintiff seeks order restraining defendant's ongoing conduct pending trial
I: Should an interlocutory injunction be granted?
R: RJR-MacDonald three-part test: serious issue, irreparable harm, balance of convenience
A: Apply each branch; consider undertaking as to damages; assess status quo
C: Grant if all three branches satisfied; order may include terms and conditions
▶ Evidence on Motions — r. 39

Primary evidence form: Affidavit evidence (r. 39.01). Affiants swear to facts within personal knowledge.

Cross-examination (r. 39.02): Any party may cross-examine an affiant on their affidavit. Cross-examination is conducted outside court, transcript filed.

Hearsay in affidavits: Permitted on motions (unlike trial) if source identified and belief stated. Court gives it less weight.

Trap: If you cross-examine on an affidavit, you are bound by the answers. You cannot later argue the affiant's evidence is unreliable without having cross-examined on it.
▶ W7: Discovery r. 30, 31
▶ Document Discovery — r. 30 Wellwood v Ontario

Affidavit of Documents (r. 30.03): Each party must serve an affidavit listing ALL documents in their possession, power, or control that are or may be relevant to issues in the action.

Three schedules:

  • Schedule A: Documents being produced (will allow inspection)
  • Schedule B: Documents being withheld (privilege claimed — must state basis)
  • Schedule C: Documents no longer in party's possession (must state what happened to them)

Wellwood v Ontario: Relevance at discovery stage is broadly construed — any document that may possibly be relevant, adversely affect a party, or support a party's case must be disclosed.

Continuing obligation: Party must supplement affidavit if new documents come into existence during litigation.

Trap: Failure to disclose a document may result in it being inadmissible at trial (r. 30.08). Court has discretion to exclude or impose conditions. Document destruction can give rise to adverse inference.
▶ Examination for Discovery — r. 31

Oral examination (r. 31.03): Each party (through representative if corporation) examined under oath. Conducted outside court.

Scope (r. 31.06): Any matter in issue or relating to a matter in issue. Includes information about witnesses' identity and location.

Undertakings: When examiner asks for information witness does not have, witness may "undertake" to get and provide it. Undertakings are binding obligations and must be fulfilled.

Refusals: Witness may refuse to answer if (1) question is improper (privileged, irrelevant, harassing); if improper refusal, opposing party may bring motion to compel.

Read-ins at trial: Admissions made at discovery may be "read in" at trial as evidence against the party who made them.

FIRAC — Discovery Refusal

F: Witness refuses to answer question at examination for discovery
I: Is the refusal proper? Can examining party compel the answer?
R: r. 31.06 broad scope; refusal only proper if: privileged, irrelevant, oppressive
A: Is the question within the scope of r. 31.06? Is a valid basis for refusal established?
C: If refusal improper: motion to compel; court may order answer and award costs against refusing party

▶ W8: Summary Judgment — r. 20 MOST IMPORTANT
▶ Hryniak v Mauldin [2014] SCC — The Test KEY CASE
Hryniak v Mauldin [2014] SCC 7 (Karakatsanis J)
"Proportionate, timely and affordable justice is the hallmark of a fair and just process."
Court must take a culture shift away from trial-centricity. Summary judgment should be the norm where it achieves a fair and just result.

The Two-Step Hryniak Test:

Step 1 — Is there a genuine issue requiring a trial?

  • Judge looks at the full record (affidavits, cross-examinations, documents)
  • No genuine issue if: (a) the claim/defence has no merit; or (b) the factual dispute can be resolved without a trial
  • Judge may weigh evidence, assess credibility, draw inferences (with enhanced fact-finding powers)

Step 2 — If genuine issue exists, can enhanced powers resolve it?

  • r. 20.04(2.1): Judge may weigh evidence and draw inferences
  • r. 20.04(2.2): Judge may order oral evidence (mini-trial) on motion
  • Use these powers if it is in the interest of justice to do so
  • Ask: will a trial be better than the motion record for reaching a just result?

FIRAC — Summary Judgment (Hryniak)

F: Defendant/plaintiff brings motion for summary judgment; argues no trial needed
I: Is there a "genuine issue requiring a trial" under r. 20?
R: r. 20.04; Hryniak two-step: (1) genuine issue? (2) enhanced powers resolve it?
A: Step 1: Review record — is there a real dispute of material fact? Step 2: Can judge weigh evidence / order oral evidence to resolve it fairly?
C: Grant SJ if: no genuine issue OR genuine issue but judge can resolve it with enhanced powers and this serves interests of justice
▶ r. 20 — Mechanics

Who can bring (r. 20.01): Plaintiff or defendant; on all or part of claim/defence.

When: After statement of defence has been filed. Usually after examination for discovery.

Responding party's obligation (r. 20.04(2)): Cannot rest on pleadings — must put its best foot forward and present specific facts showing genuine issue for trial.

Partial summary judgment: Court may grant SJ on some issues and order trial on remaining issues.

Trap: If responding party has not conducted discovery and needs more time, may ask court to adjourn under r. 20.05 ("need more time to get evidence"). Court has discretion to allow this but will not automatically do so.
▶ W9: Trial Procedure r. 52, 53, 57
▶ Trial Mechanics — r. 52

Order of proceedings at trial:

  • (1) Plaintiff's opening statement
  • (2) Plaintiff's witnesses (examination in chief → cross-examination → re-examination)
  • (3) Defendant's witnesses (same sequence)
  • (4) Plaintiff may call reply evidence
  • (5) Closing submissions (defendant first, then plaintiff)
  • (6) Judgment

Burden of proof: Plaintiff bears burden on balance of probabilities for each element of the claim. Defendant bears burden on affirmative defences.

Non-suit (r. 52.03): After plaintiff's case, defendant may move for non-suit without calling evidence — argues plaintiff has not established a prima facie case.

Trap: Once defendant elects to call evidence, right to non-suit is lost. Defendant must elect before calling any evidence.
▶ Expert Evidence — r. 53

Expert's duty (r. 53.03): Expert's primary duty is to the court, not the party who retained them. Must provide fair, objective, and non-partisan opinion.

Requirements for expert opinion:

  • Expert report must be served at least 90 days before trial (60 days for responding expert)
  • Report must set out: qualifications, facts relied on, opinion, and reasoning
  • Expert must acknowledge duty to court (signed acknowledgment)

Mohan criteria (admissibility): (1) Relevance; (2) Necessity; (3) Absence of exclusionary rule; (4) Properly qualified expert.

Trap: Failure to serve expert report in time may mean expert cannot testify at trial (r. 53.03(3)). Court has discretion to allow late service but may impose costs or other conditions.
▶ Costs — r. 57 Waxman v Waxman

General principle: Costs follow the event — the losing party pays the winning party's costs (partial indemnity).

Scales of costs:

  • Partial indemnity: Default; approximately 50-60% of actual costs
  • Substantial indemnity: Approximately 80-90%; awarded when party conduct warrants (bad faith, improper conduct)
  • Full indemnity: Very rare; egregious conduct

Waxman v Waxman: Court has broad discretion on costs. Factors include: success, offers to settle, conduct of parties, complexity, proportionality of legal fees.

Offers to settle (r. 49): If plaintiff fails to beat defendant's offer, defendant may be entitled to partial indemnity costs up to offer date, and substantial indemnity after. Creates powerful settlement incentive.

Trap: Classification as interlocutory vs final is determined by the effect on the rights, not the form of the order. A summary judgment dismissing an action is a final order even though made without trial.
▶ Appellate Procedure — r. 61

Notice of Appeal (r. 61.04): Filed and served within 30 days of final order. Perfected within 60 days.

Appeal record: Appellant must perfect appeal by filing: (1) appeal book and compendium; (2) transcript of evidence; (3) factum.

Factum: Written argument outlining facts, issues, law, and relief sought. Appellant's factum due first; respondent responds; appellant may reply.

Fresh evidence on appeal (Palmer test): New evidence generally not admissible on appeal unless: (1) not available with reasonable diligence at trial; (2) relevant and credible; (3) could reasonably have affected the result.

Andreacchi v Perruccio: Appellate court must show deference to trial judge's findings of fact. Will not interfere unless palpable (obvious) and overriding (material to the result) error shown.

FIRAC — Standard of Review on Appeal

F: Appellant challenges trial judge's findings
I: What is the appropriate standard of review?
R: Law = correctness; fact = palpable & overriding error; discretion = error in principle
A: Characterize the finding: is it a pure legal conclusion or a factual finding?
C: Apply correct standard; intervention warranted only if standard met
▶ W11: Class Proceedings CPA 1992
▶ Class Proceedings Act 1992 — Certification Blanchette v Squires

Five certification requirements (CPA s. 5):

  • (1) Cause of action: Pleadings must disclose a cause of action (same as r. 21 standard)
  • (2) Identifiable class: Class must be capable of objective definition; two or more persons
  • (3) Common issues: Claims raise common issues of fact or law (need not predominate)
  • (4) Preferable procedure: Class proceeding is preferable to individual actions for resolving common issues (Blanchette)
  • (5) Representative plaintiff: Named plaintiff adequately represents class interests; no conflict; litigation plan
Blanchette v Squires — Preferable Procedure: Must consider whether class proceeding offers advantages over individual suits in terms of: access to justice, behaviour modification, judicial economy. Not a merits assessment at certification.

FIRAC — Certification of Class Action

F: Proposed representative plaintiff seeks certification of class action
I: Does the proceeding meet the five certification requirements under CPA s. 5?
R: CPA 1992 s. 5; Blanchette on preferable procedure
A: Check each of the five requirements; focus on common issues and preferable procedure
C: If all five satisfied: certify; identify representative plaintiff, class definition, and common issues
▶ Class Proceedings — Procedure

Opt-out regime: Class members are automatically included unless they opt out within the specified period. Opt-in is used in some jurisdictions but not Ontario.

Common issues trial: After certification, court determines common issues applicable to all class members. Individual issues (e.g., individual damages) assessed separately.

Notice: Court approves notice to class members. Must describe class, common issues, right to opt out, litigation plan.

Costs: Representative plaintiff is not personally liable for defendant's costs unless proceeding was frivolous or vexatious (CPA s. 31).

Trap: Ontario v Rothmans — certification is not a merits determination. Do not argue the case is unlikely to succeed at certification stage. Threshold is low.
▶ W12: Alternative Dispute Resolution r. 24.1, 49
▶ Mandatory Mediation — r. 24.1 Jackson v Surka

Scope: Applies in Toronto, Ottawa, and Essex regions to defended actions (excluding family, estates, and certain other proceedings). Mandatory under r. 24.1.

Process:

  • Parties must attend mediation within 180 days of first defence
  • Mediator selected from approved roster, or parties may agree on any person
  • All parties and their counsel must attend
  • Mediator facilitates — does not adjudicate

Confidentiality: Statements made in mediation are without prejudice and not admissible at trial (r. 24.1.14). Mediation is privileged.

Jackson v Surka: Party who refuses to attend mediation or does not participate in good faith may face cost consequences. Good faith participation is required.

Trap: Failure to comply with mandatory mediation timelines may result in the action being stayed until mediation is completed. Cannot proceed to trial without certificate of non-settlement (or agreement) from mediator.
▶ Offers to Settle — r. 49

Rule 49 offer mechanism: A party may serve a written offer to settle at any time. The offer may be withdrawn any time before acceptance (unless stated to be open for a specific time).

Cost consequences:

  • Plaintiff's offer — not beaten: If plaintiff gets judgment less favourable than their own offer, plaintiff entitled only to partial indemnity costs up to offer date; defendant entitled to partial indemnity costs after
  • Defendant's offer — not beaten: If plaintiff's judgment is no better than defendant's offer, defendant entitled to partial indemnity costs from offer date onward

Purpose: Encourages early settlement. Creates cost risk for party who refuses a reasonable offer.

Strategy: Always make a r. 49 offer early in litigation. Even if it is not accepted, it shifts costs risk to the other side if you do at least as well as your offer.

FIRAC — Rule 49 Cost Consequences

F: Defendant offered to settle for $X; plaintiff rejected; plaintiff recovered $Y at trial
I: What are the cost consequences under r. 49?
R: r. 49.10: if judgment no more favourable than offer → defendant gets partial indemnity costs after offer date
A: Compare judgment amount to offer; which is more favourable? Date offer was made?
C: Allocate costs: partial indemnity before offer date; defendant's costs on partial indemnity after offer date if offer not beaten
▶ Settlement Conferences & Arbitration

Pre-trial conference (r. 50): Mandatory before all trials. Judge (not the trial judge) hears summaries of positions, identifies issues, may encourage settlement. Pre-trial briefs filed in advance.

Judicial dispute resolution (JDR): Voluntary process where parties meet with a judge for binding or non-binding resolution. Available in Ontario through various programs.

Arbitration: Parties agree to submit dispute to private arbitrator whose decision is binding. Governed by Ontario Arbitration Act, 1991. Court may stay a lawsuit where parties have an arbitration agreement (s. 7).

Arbitration vs. Mediation: Arbitrator decides the dispute (adjudicative). Mediator facilitates agreement (facilitative). Arbitration award is enforceable as a court judgment.
Trap: Courts are reluctant to grant stays in favour of arbitration where one party is a consumer (Arbitration Act consumer protection provisions) or where the dispute is not within the arbitration clause's scope.

▶ Common Traps & Exam Mistakes Warning
Top 10 Civil Procedure Traps:

1. Forgetting r. 1.04: Every procedure answer should open with the proportionality principle.

2. Deemed admissions (r. 25.07): If defence doesn't specifically deny an allegation, it is admitted. Examiners love this.

3. Hryniak Step 2: Don't stop at Step 1 of summary judgment. Even if there IS a genuine issue, ask if enhanced powers can resolve it.

4. Interlocutory vs final order: Determines whether appeal goes to Divisional Court or Court of Appeal. Don't confuse these.

5. Limitation period is a defence: Must be pleaded by defendant; court cannot raise it. Check timing carefully.

6. Substitutional service: Not automatic. Must show genuine attempts at personal service failed.

7. r. 21 motion: No evidence. Plain and obvious standard. Court assumes pleaded facts are true. Cannot test credibility.

8. Solicitor-client vs litigation privilege: Litigation privilege ends when litigation ends; solicitor-client privilege is permanent.

9. r. 49 offer cost consequences: Check whether the offer was made by plaintiff or defendant, and whether the judgment was more or less favourable than the offer.

10. Non-suit election: Once defendant calls any evidence, the right to move for non-suit is forever lost.
