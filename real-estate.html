▶ 1A. Stages of a Real Estate Transaction Rule

7 Stages (memorize this sequence):

1. Offer / Negotiation — parties negotiate APS terms
2. Agreement of Purchase and Sale (APS) — binding contract formed
3. Due Diligence / Searches — title search, off-title searches, survey review
4. Requisitions — buyer raises objections; vendor must answer
5. Pre-Closing — mortgage arrangements, document preparation
6. Closing — exchange of documents, keys, funds
7. Post-Closing — registration, reporting to client, undertakings

Exam tip: Every exam question maps to one or more of these stages. Identify the stage FIRST before analyzing the legal issue.
▶ Role of Law Clerk vs Lawyer

Law Clerk CAN: Conduct searches, prepare documents, draft requisition letters, calculate adjustments, arrange registrations

Law Clerk CANNOT: Give legal advice, sign opinions on title, exercise professional judgment, appear in court, supervise other lawyers

Exam trap: If a fact pattern shows a clerk advising a client on whether title is "good" — that is unauthorized practice of law. Only the lawyer can opine on title quality.

▶ 1C. Easements Test

Definition: A right to use another's land for a specific purpose. Does NOT give possession — only use.

Dominant tenement: Land that BENEFITS from the easement
Servient tenement: Land that is BURDENED by the easement

▶ Methods of Creation

1. Express Grant: Created by deed or instrument registered on title

2. Prescription (20 years): Open, notorious, continuous, adverse use for 20+ years without permission. Only in Registry system (NOT Land Titles — LT protects registered owner).

3. Implied by Law: Easement of necessity (e.g., landlocked parcel); easement by prior use (Wheeldon v Burrows)

4. Statute: Utility easements, drainage rights, etc.

Exam trap: Prescriptive easements do NOT arise in Land Titles. If the question says the property is in the LT system, prescription fails.
▶ Restrictive Covenants

Definition: A negative promise restricting land use (e.g., "no commercial use"). Runs with the land if:

1. There is a dominant and servient tenement
2. The covenant "touches and concerns" the dominant land
3. The covenant was intended to run with the land
4. The purchaser had NOTICE (actual, constructive, or by registration)

Equity rule: Restrictive covenants are enforced in equity. An innocent purchaser without notice takes free of an unregistered covenant (in Registry). In Land Titles, only registered interests bind.

FIRAC — Easement Dispute

F: [Identify dominant/servient tenement; describe the alleged use; duration of use; title system]
I: "The issue is whether a valid easement exists over the servient tenement."
R: An easement requires a dominant and servient tenement, accommodation of the dominant tenement, and proper creation (express grant, prescription 20yr, implied by necessity). Prescription unavailable in Land Titles. Express grants must be registered.
A: [Apply creation method to facts; check title system; check registration]

▶ 1D. Adverse Possession Rule

Requirements (10-year period):

1. Actual possession — physical occupation/use
2. Open and notorious — visible to the true owner
3. Exclusive — not shared with true owner
4. Continuous — unbroken for the statutory period
5. Adverse/hostile — without permission of the true owner

Real Property Limitations Act: 10-year limitation period

Critical: Adverse possession does NOT apply to Land Titles properties (government guarantee protects registered owners). Only applies in Registry system. LTCQ conversions extinguish possessory claims unless noted on conversion.
▶ 1E. Mineral Rights & Riparian Rights

Mineral Rights: Surface owner does not necessarily own subsurface minerals. Crown reservations common. Always check parcel register for mineral rights reservations.

Riparian Rights: Owners of land bordering water have right of access to the water, but do not own the water itself. Accretion (gradual) adds to owner's land; avulsion (sudden) does not.

▶ Matrimonial Home — FLA s. 18-21 Key

FLA s. 18: Every property ordinarily occupied by spouses as their family residence IS a matrimonial home.

FLA s. 21(1): No spouse shall dispose of or encumber an interest in a matrimonial home unless the other spouse joins or consents.

Mamando v Singh: Two-step inquiry: (1) Is there a spouse? (2) Is the property a matrimonial home? BOTH must be answered before requiring consent.

Stoimenov v Stoimenov: Constructive notice — purchaser should have known property was a matrimonial home despite sole ownership on title.

Maroukis v Maroukis [SCC]: An execution (judgment) registered against a spouse is NOT an "encumbrance" for FLA purposes — spousal consent is still required for disposition.

Exam trap: Even if only ONE spouse is on title, the non-titled spouse has a RIGHT TO POSSESSION and must consent to sale. Failure to obtain consent = transaction can be set aside.

FIRAC — Matrimonial Home / Spousal Consent

F: [Identify: married? property used as family residence? who is on title? consent obtained?]
I: "The issue is whether spousal consent under FLA s. 21 was required for this disposition."
R: Under FLA s. 18, a matrimonial home is any property ordinarily occupied as the family residence. Under s. 21(1), no spouse may dispose of or encumber an interest without the other spouse joining or consenting. Per Mamando v Singh, apply a two-step inquiry: (1) is there a spouse? (2) is the property a matrimonial home? Per Stoimenov, constructive notice applies.
A: [Apply two-step test to facts; check if consent was obtained; check if purchaser had notice]
C: "Therefore, the disposition [is/is not] valid because..."
▶ Death / Survivorship / Estate Conveyancing

Joint Tenancy + Death: Survivor normally takes by right of survivorship. BUT FLA s. 26(1) deems JT severed on death of a spouse — share goes to estate, not survivor.

Estate conveyancing: Personal representative (executor/administrator) conveys. Need: Certificate of Appointment of Estate Trustee, death certificate, clearance certificate (Estate Administration Tax Act).

Exam trap: FLA s. 26(1) severance ONLY applies to spouses (married). Common-law partners → normal survivorship rules apply.
▶ Age, Corporations, Partnerships & Trustees

Minors: Conveyance by a minor is VOID (not voidable). Cannot contract for land.

Corporations: Must have capacity (objects clause / articles). Indoor management rule: outsiders can assume corporate officers acted within authority unless they knew otherwise.

Partnerships: Partnership property held by partners as tenants in common (Partnership Act). One partner can bind the firm for transactions in the ordinary course.

Trustees: Must have authority under the trust instrument. Check for trustee's powers of sale. Purchaser not bound to see to application of purchase money (Trustee Act).

▶ Land Titles Qualifications — s. 44(1)

Even LT title is subject to:

1. Provincial Crown interests • 2. Municipal taxes/charges • 3. Highway/railway rights • 4. Leases < 3 years with tenant in possession • 5. Public rights of way • 6. Any rights of expropriation

These are the "statutory exceptions" to the government guarantee. Always check s. 44(1) qualifications when analyzing LT title.
▶ LT Conversion Categories: LTCQ / LT Absolute / LT Plus

▶ POLARIS / Teraview & Bill 152 (Anti-Fraud) Case Law

POLARIS: Province Of Lands And Registration Information System — electronic database for all Ontario land records.

Teraview: Software for electronic searching and registration; used by all Ontario lawyers.

Bill 152 (2006): Amended LTA to adopt deferred indefeasibility to combat fraud.

Lawrence v Wright: Fraudster forged transfer to themselves, then sold to innocent purchaser Wright. Held: deferred indefeasibility — the immediate purchaser FROM the fraudster (Wright) gets NO protection. Only a SUBSEQUENT innocent purchaser (one more step removed) would be protected.

Deferred Indefeasibility Rule:
Fraudster → Immediate Purchaser = NO protection (title can be restored to true owner)
Fraudster → Immediate Purchaser → Subsequent Purchaser = PROTECTED (if for value, without notice of fraud)

FIRAC — Title System / Fraud

F: [Identify title system; describe the alleged fraud; who are the parties in the chain?]
I: "The issue is whether the purchaser is protected under the title system despite the fraud."
R: Under the Land Titles system, title is guaranteed by the government (LTA s. 140(2)). However, per Lawrence v Wright and Bill 152, Ontario follows deferred indefeasibility: the immediate purchaser from a fraudster is NOT protected; only subsequent purchasers for value without notice are protected. In Registry, nemo dat applies — a forged instrument passes nothing.
A: [Is the purchaser immediate or subsequent? Did they pay value? Did they have notice?]
C: "Therefore, the purchaser [is/is not] protected because..."
▶ 2D. Title Insurance Tip

What it covers: Title defects, fraud/forgery, survey deficiencies, zoning non-compliance, unregistered easements, encroachments, work orders

What it does NOT cover: Known defects disclosed before policy; environmental contamination (usually); matters arising after policy date

Rule 3.2-9.4 (Rules of Professional Conduct): Lawyer must advise client of option of title insurance but cannot require it as a substitute for a proper title search.

Practice point: Title insurance has largely replaced the need for up-to-date surveys in residential transactions, but the lawyer still has a duty to do proper due diligence.
▶ 3A. Fixtures vs Chattels Test

Why it matters: Fixtures pass with the land automatically; chattels do NOT (must be specifically included in APS).

▶ Stack v T Eaton — 5-Rule Test Case Law

The 5 rules (memorize):

1. Degree of annexation: How firmly is the item attached? (nails, screws, bolts vs resting by own weight)
2. Purpose of annexation: Was it attached to enhance the land OR for the better use of the chattel itself?
3. Damage on removal: Would removal cause substantial damage to the property?
4. Tenant vs Owner: Tenants' fixtures have more liberal removal rights (trade fixtures exception)
5. Agreement of the parties: Express agreement in APS overrides the common law test

▶ Royal Bank v Maple Ridge — 6 Additional Rules Case Law

Expanded the Stack test with additional considerations:

1. Nature of the item • 2. Manner of attachment • 3. Damage on removal • 4. Nature of the premises • 5. Intention of the annexor • 6. Relationship of the parties (landlord-tenant vs vendor-purchaser)

▶ Bernard v Weiss Case Law

Applied multi-factor test; confirmed that intention of the annexor is important but must be inferred from objective facts, not subjective statements.

FIRAC — Fixtures vs Chattels

F: [Describe the item; how attached; who attached it; any APS clause?]
I: "The issue is whether [item] is a fixture (passing with the land) or a chattel (retained by vendor)."
R: Per Stack v T Eaton, apply the 5-rule test: (1) degree of annexation, (2) purpose of annexation, (3) damage on removal, (4) tenant vs owner, (5) agreement. Per Royal Bank v Maple Ridge, also consider the nature of the premises and relationship of the parties. Per Bernard v Weiss, intention is inferred objectively.
A: [Apply each factor to the facts]

▶ 3B. Land Description Issues in APS Case Law

Turney v Zhilka [SCC]: APS must sufficiently describe the land to satisfy Statute of Frauds. Conditions precedent that are TRUE CPs (dependent on outside events, not in party's control) do not make the contract void for uncertainty — contract is contingent.

Dynamic Transport v OK Detailing [SCC]: Court can look at the factual matrix (surrounding circumstances) to interpret an ambiguous land description. Parol evidence admissible to identify the property, not to vary the contract.

LeMesurier v Andrus — Substantiality Test: If a land description error creates a substantial deficiency (significant difference between what was described and what actually exists), the purchaser can rescind. Minor discrepancies → abatement of purchase price instead.

MacIsaac v Salo: Court has power to correct a misdescription where the parties' intent is clear and there is no prejudice to third parties.

FIRAC — Defective Land Description

F: [What does the APS say? What does the survey show? How big is the discrepancy?]
I: "The issue is whether the land description in the APS is sufficient to create a binding contract."
R: The Statute of Frauds requires a written description sufficient to identify the land. Per Dynamic Transport, courts may look at the factual matrix to resolve ambiguity. Per LeMesurier v Andrus, a substantial deficiency entitles the buyer to rescind; minor deficiency → abatement. Per MacIsaac v Salo, courts can correct misdescription if intent is clear.
A: [Is the error substantial or minor? Can the factual matrix resolve it?]
C: "Therefore, the contract [is/is not] enforceable because..."

▶ Promises vs Conditions vs Warranties Key

▶ Conditions Precedent — Turney v Zhilka Case Law

Turney v Zhilka [SCC]: Distinguishes:

True Condition Precedent: Depends on an event outside BOTH parties' control (e.g., rezoning approval). If not met, contract simply does not come into force. Neither party is in breach.

Promissory Condition: One party promises to USE BEST EFFORTS to fulfill (e.g., "subject to buyer obtaining financing"). Party must make good faith effort; failure to try = breach.

Exam trap: A "subject to" clause is NOT automatically a true CP. Ask: can either party control the outcome? If yes → promissory condition, and that party must make best efforts.

Tse v Sood — 4 Grounds to Refuse Closing:

1. Defective title — vendor cannot deliver good title
2. Breach of contract — material breach of APS term
3. Failure of condition precedent — CP not satisfied
4. Misrepresentation — vendor made false statement buyer relied upon

▶ Time of the Essence Rule

Meaning: The closing date is STRICT — failure to close on time = breach entitling the other party to terminate.

OREA Para 21: "Time shall in all respects be of the essence."

Salama v Grewal: Where time is of the essence, even a one-day delay = repudiatory breach.

King v Urban: Confirmed strictness of time-of-the-essence clauses; party must be ready, willing, and able to close on the date.

Tender: To enforce time of the essence, the non-breaching party must TENDER (make themselves ready, willing, and able to perform). Per Zhilka v Turney, you cannot claim the other side breached if you yourself were not ready to close.
▶ 3D. Title Search & Due Diligence Rule

Title Search Steps (Land Titles):

1. Search parcel register on Teraview • 2. Check registered owner matches vendor • 3. Review all registered instruments (charges, easements, liens) • 4. Check s. 44(1) qualifications • 5. Check LTCQ qualifier if applicable

Title Search Steps (Registry):

1. Search 40-year chain of title • 2. Identify root of title (good conveyance for value) • 3. Trace every instrument in the chain • 4. Check for breaks in chain • 5. Check Registry Act compliance

Off-Title Searches: Municipal (zoning, building permits, work orders) • Executions • Corporate status • Tax certificates • Utility companies • Conservation authority • Planning Act compliance

Planning Act Search: Verify that all prior conveyances complied with Planning Act subdivision control (s. 50). Non-compliant transfers are VOID.

▶ 3E. Requisitions Statute

Definition: Written demands by buyer's lawyer to vendor's lawyer to fix title defects or comply with APS terms.

Categories:

1. Title requisitions — defects on the face of title (encumbrances, liens, breaks in chain)
2. Root of title requisitions — problems with the root deed (Registry only)
3. Conveyance requisitions — issues with the form of transfer documents
4. Contract requisitions — vendor not complying with APS terms

Sources: Title search results • Off-title search results • Survey review • APS terms themselves

Procedure: Submit within requisition period (OREA Para 11) • Vendor must answer satisfactorily • Buyer must follow up before closing • Unanswered requisitions = buyer may refuse to close

Exam trap: Missing the requisition deadline waives the right to requisition on most matters (but NOT on matters going to root of title or fraud).
▶ 4A. Charge/Mortgage Creation & Types Statute

Three ways to create a charge:

1. New mortgage: Purchaser borrows from lender; charge registered on title at closing
2. Assumed mortgage: Purchaser takes over vendor's existing mortgage (lender must consent)
3. Vendor take-back (VTB): Vendor acts as lender; takes charge back from purchaser as part of purchase price

▶ 4B. Priority of Charges Rule

General rule: Priority determined by ORDER OF REGISTRATION (first in time, first in right).

Exceptions:

• Construction liens — may have priority over prior mortgages to extent of "improvement"
• Property taxes — always have priority (statutory super-priority)
• Subordination agreement — parties can agree to change priority
• Tacking — further advances under existing mortgage maintain original priority if obligatory

Practical effect: First mortgage has first claim on proceeds of sale. If property sells for less than total debt, second mortgagee may recover nothing.
▶ 4C. Default Remedies Key
RemedyProcessKey Points
Power of Sale15 days notice of default + 35 days after notice = 50 days total before saleMost common remedy; no court order needed; mortgagee sells property; surplus to mortgagor; if deficiency, personal judgment

Exam trap: Power of sale = 15 + 35 days (memorize these numbers). Foreclosure extinguishes ALL equity — so if the property is worth more than the debt, foreclosure hurts the mortgagor more.
▶ 4D. Equity of Redemption & Key Cases Case Law

Equity of Redemption: The mortgagor's right to redeem the property by paying the full amount owing, at any time before the property is sold or foreclosure order is final.

Kreglinger v New Patagonia Meat [HL]: Cannot clog the equity of redemption. Any mortgage term that makes it impossible or impractical to redeem is void. Collateral advantages that survive the mortgage are suspect. A mortgage must remain redeemable.

Rule against clogs: (1) No postponement making redemption illusory; (2) No collateral advantage surviving redemption; (3) Mortgage must always be redeemable on payment of principal + interest + costs.
▶ Interest Act s. 6 — Ferland v Sun Life

Interest Act s. 6: Any mortgage for a term exceeding 5 years may be prepaid after 5 years on payment of 3 months' bonus interest. This is a federal statutory right that overrides the contract.

Ferland v Sun Life: Confirmed that s. 6 applies to all mortgages with terms > 5 years, regardless of what the contract says. Corporate borrowers may be excluded by agreement.

▶ Assignment — Lee v Korea Exchange Bank

Assignment of mortgage: Mortgagee can assign the mortgage to a third party. Mortgagor's obligations continue unchanged.

Lee v Korea Exchange Bank: Assignee takes subject to all equities — any defences the mortgagor had against the original mortgagee are available against the assignee.

▶ Independent Legal Advice (ILA) Requirements

When required: Guarantor/surety signing a mortgage; spouse signing a charge on matrimonial home; any party in a potentially disadvantageous position.

Requirements: Separate lawyer • Explain nature and consequences of document • Ensure voluntary signing • Certificate of ILA

Exam trap: If a spouse signs a mortgage without ILA, the charge may be set aside. Always flag ILA issues when a non-borrowing spouse is involved.

▶ 5A. Closing the Transaction Rule
▶ Acting for Vendor

Key tasks:

• Prepare Statement of Adjustments (taxes, utilities, rent, fuel; prorated to closing date)
• Calculate Land Transfer Tax (LTT) for purchaser
• Prepare Transfer/Deed (Form 1 in LT, deed in Registry)
• Obtain discharge of existing mortgage or arrange payout
• Provide statutory declarations (re: possession, no improvements affecting boundaries, FLA compliance)
• Prepare direction re: title (if vendor wants title in different name than APS)

▶ Acting for Purchaser

Key tasks:

• Complete title search and off-title searches
• Submit requisitions and follow up
• Verify zoning compliance — Wong v Hassard: lawyer liable for failure to check zoning
• Arrange mortgage financing (new charge, commitment letter)
• Review and approve closing documents
• Calculate and pay LTT on closing
• Register transfer and charge electronically (Teraview/DRA)

Wong v Hassard: Lawyer failed to verify that the property's use complied with zoning. Held liable for negligence. ALWAYS check zoning as part of due diligence.
▶ LTT Calculation

Ontario LTT rates (cumulative brackets):

• 0.5% on first $55,000
• 1.0% on $55,001 – $250,000
• 1.5% on $250,001 – $400,000
• 2.0% on $400,001 – $2,000,000
• 2.5% on amounts over $2,000,000 (for land with 1-2 single family residences)

First-time home buyer refund: Up to $4,000 rebate (on first $368,000 of value)

Toronto: Additional municipal LTT at same rates (doubled total LTT). First-time buyer rebate up to $4,475 for municipal LTT.

Document Registration Agreement (DRA): Lawyers agree on protocol for electronic closing — who registers what and when; obligations if something goes wrong post-registration.

E-reg process: Both lawyers prepare documents in Teraview • Purchaser's lawyer registers transfer + charge simultaneously • Vendor's lawyer registers discharge of old mortgage • Funds flow upon confirmation of registration

▶ 5B. Good Title & Permitted Encumbrances Case Law

Frank Street v Caravatta: "Good title" means title free from reasonable doubt — a willing buyer should not have to accept title that a court might later declare defective. It is NOT enough that title is "arguable" — it must be clear.

Clements v Wyatt: Vendor must show clear, marketable title; purchaser not obliged to accept title depending on "events that might or might not happen."

Green v Kaufman: Vendor who cannot deliver good title is in breach regardless of fault.

Permitted encumbrances (OREA s. 10): APS typically provides that purchaser accepts title subject to "any registered restrictions, conditions, or covenants that run with the land provided they have been complied with." Buyer can still requisition if encumbrance was NOT disclosed.

Work orders / open permits: These are NOT registered on title but can be discovered through municipal searches. An outstanding work order is a title defect that entitles the buyer to refuse to close.
▶ 5C. Remedies for Breach Key
▶ Specific Performance Case Law

Semelhago v Paramadevan [SCC]: Specific performance (SP) is available only if:

1. The property is unique (not just any real property — must be truly unique to the buyer), AND
2. Damages are inadequate to compensate the buyer

The SCC rejected the old presumption that all land is unique. The plaintiff must PROVE uniqueness.

Southcott v TCDSB: Commercial/investment properties are generally NOT unique — damages are the normal remedy.

John E Dodge: SP may be available where property has special characteristics making substitution impossible.

SP with abatement: Where title is slightly defective but buyer still wants the property, court may order SP with a reduction in purchase price to account for the defect.

Post-Semelhago framework: (1) Is this a residential home buyer? → likely unique. (2) Investment/commercial property? → likely NOT unique → damages. (3) Can the buyer find a substitute? If yes → damages adequate.
▶ Damages & Deposits Case Law

642947 Ontario v Fleischer: Measure of damages = difference between contract price and market value at date of breach, plus consequential losses (reasonable moving costs, etc.).

HW Liebig v Leading Investment [SCC]: Deposit = earnest money. If buyer defaults, deposit is forfeited to vendor as liquidated damages (not a penalty). Vendor keeps deposit regardless of actual loss.

Deposit vs down payment: Deposit is forfeited on default (liquidated damages per Liebig). Down payment is only a partial payment — governed by normal damages rules. The APS should clearly state which it is.

Mason v Freedman [SCC]: Vendor cannot capriciously refuse to complete and then claim the other side breached. Good faith obligation applies.

Bhasin v Hrynew [SCC]: Organizing principle of good faith in contract performance; duty of honest performance. Applies to real estate transactions.

▶ Rescission vs Repudiation Case Law

Guarantee Co v Gordon Capital [SCC]: Critical distinction:

Rescission (ab initio)Repudiation (Termination)
EffectContract treated as if it NEVER EXISTED; parties restored to pre-contract positionContract terminated going forward; rights that accrued before termination survive
GroundsFraud, fundamental mistake, misrepresentation (innocent or negligent)Breach of condition, repudiatory breach, anticipatory breach
TimingPre-closing only (cannot rescind after title has passed, except for fraud)Can occur at any time upon breach
DepositReturned to buyer (contract unwound)Depends on who breached — forfeited if buyer breached (Liebig)
Exam trap: After closing (title has passed), rescission is generally NOT available — damages only. EXCEPTION: fraud allows post-closing rescission.

▶ 5D. Tender & Time of the Essence (Remedies Context) Rule

Salama v Grewal: Where time is of the essence, party must be ready to close on the date. Even ONE DAY late = repudiatory breach. The innocent party can accept the repudiation and terminate.

King v Urban: Confirmed: time of the essence clauses are strictly enforced. If you want to extend, must get written agreement from the other side.

Tender requirement: To claim the other party breached, YOU must tender (show you were ready, willing, and able to close). You cannot claim breach while you yourself were not ready.

Exam approach: Whenever a question involves a missed closing date: (1) Is time of the essence? (2) Who was ready to close? (3) Did the ready party tender? If yes to all → the non-ready party is in breach.
