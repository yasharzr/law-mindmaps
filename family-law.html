▶ 1B. Dispute Resolution Processes Statute

Spectrum (least → most adversarial):

1. Negotiation → 2. Mediation → 3. Collaborative Law → 4. Arbitration → 5. Litigation

DA s. 7.3: Parties shall try FDR process (unless inappropriate).

DA s. 7.7(2): Lawyers MUST encourage FDR and inform of family justice services.

Exception: Where family violence exists, mediation/collaboration may be inappropriate due to power imbalance.

▶ 1C. Family Violence Case Law

Definition (DA s. 2(1)): Violent, threatening, or coercive/controlling conduct causing fear or harm.

Includes: Physical abuse, sexual abuse, psychological abuse, coercive control (verbal abuse, isolation, financial control, false allegations), exposure of child to violence.

Ahluwalia v Ahluwalia (2022 ONSC 1303) — Established tort of family violence; coercive control = actionable civil harm.

FV is a CROSS-CUTTING issue. It affects:
• Dispute Resolution → FDR may be inappropriate
• Property → Affects negotiation of agreements
• Support → May affect self-sufficiency
• Agreements → May ground unconscionability/duress
• Parenting → s. 16(3)(j) mandatory factor
• Child Support → May affect enforcement
• Relocation → Victim may need to move for safety

▶ Essential Validity (Capacity) — 6 Federal Requirements

1. Two people — Hyde v Hyde (1866) originally "one man one woman" → Halpern v Toronto (2003 ONCA) struck down → Reference re Same-Sex Marriage (2004 SCC) "living tree" → Civil Marriage Act (2005): "two persons"

2. Consent (voluntary + informed) — Tanti v Tanti (2020): consent + understanding required. M.A. v B.B. (2018): fraud can vitiate. Grewal v Kaur (2009): forced marriages.

3. Ability to consummate (voidable if unable) — Rae v Rae (1944): incapacity not mere refusal. Kaur v Singh (2021 BCCA).

4. Outside prohibited degrees — Consanguinity (blood) + Affinity (marriage-created). Marriage (Prohibited Degrees) Act, 1990.

5. No prior existing marriage (no bigamy) — Larsen, Re (1980).

6. Appropriate age — Province sets minimum (typically 18; 16 with consent).

▶ Formal Validity (Solemnization) — Provincial

Licence or permit • Authority of marriage officer • Parental consent (if minor) • Witnesses • Registration

Cure for formal defects = Good Faith — Lalonde v Agha (2021): If parties acted in good faith, formal defect doesn't void marriage.

I: "The issue is whether a valid marriage exists."
R: Marriage requires essential validity (federal: 2 persons, consent, consummation ability, outside prohibited degrees, no prior marriage, age) AND formal validity (provincial: licence, officer, witnesses). Void = never existed; voidable = valid until annulled. Good faith cures formal defects (Lalonde v Agha).
A: [Check each requirement against facts]
C: "Therefore, the marriage is [valid/void/voidable] because..."
▶ 2A. Grounds for Divorce — DA s. 8(1) Statute

Breakdown of marriage (3 grounds):

1. Separation ≥ 1 year s. 8(2)(a) ← 94.6% of all divorces use this
• Living "separate and apart" (can be under same roof)
• 90-day reconciliation attempt allowed without restarting clock
• Date of separation = triggers valuation date for property

2. Adultery s. 8(2)(b) ← rarely used; cannot use own adultery

3. Cruelty s. 8(2)(b)(ii) ← rarely used
• Physical/mental cruelty making cohabitation intolerable
• McPhail v McPhail (2001 BCCA): Courts SHOULD use separation ground — cruelty inflames hostility, serves no practical purpose

▶ 2B. Religious Barriers — DA s. 21.1 Case Law

Marcovitz v Bruker (2007 SCC): Husband promised GET (Jewish divorce) then refused → $60,000 damages. Court can refuse to process divorce if party won't remove religious barriers.

▶ FIRAC Template — Divorce Template
I: "The issue is whether [party] is entitled to a divorce."
R: Under DA s. 8(1), divorce requires proof of marriage breakdown. The most common ground is separation ≥ 1 year (s. 8(2)(a)). Per McPhail, courts prefer separation over fault grounds. 90-day reconciliation attempt does not restart the clock.
A: [When did they separate? 1+ year apart? Any reconciliation?]
C: "Therefore, divorce [will/will not] be granted because..."

▶ 3B. Unjust Enrichment & JFV (Common-Law) Test
▶ Unjust Enrichment — 3-Part Test (Kerr v Baranow, 2011 SCC)

1. ENRICHMENT of the defendant — Did they receive a benefit? (money, services, property appreciation)

2. CORRESPONDING DEPRIVATION of the claimant — Did claimant suffer a loss? Domestic services NOT presumed gratuitous.

3. NO JURISTIC REASON — Two-stage analysis:
  Stage 1: Claimant shows no established reason (contract, gift, statute, disposition of law)
  Stage 2: Defendant points to residual policy reasons to allow enrichment

If all 3 met → Remedy:
(a) Monetary award (preferred if quantifiable)
(b) Constructive trust (proprietary — if monetary inadequate; requires link to specific property)

▶ Joint Family Venture — 4-Factor Test

1. MUTUAL EFFORT — Did both contribute to shared goals?

2. ECONOMIC INTEGRATION — Were finances merged/shared?

3. ACTUAL INTENT — Did they intend a joint enterprise?

4. FAMILY PRIORITY — Did family needs drive decisions?

If JFV exists → Remedy = proportionate share of accumulated wealth (NOT automatic 50/50).

Vanasse v Seguin: $8.5M vs $322K → JFV gave proportionate share.

I: "The issue is whether [claimant] has an equitable property interest."
R: Under Kerr v Baranow (2011 SCC), unjust enrichment requires: (1) enrichment, (2) corresponding deprivation, (3) no juristic reason. If established, remedy is monetary or constructive trust. Where parties operated as a JFV (mutual effort, economic integration, actual intent, family priority), remedy is proportionate share. Domestic services are NOT presumed gratuitous.
A: [Go through each element with facts; assess JFV factors]
C: "Therefore, [claimant] is [entitled/not entitled] to [monetary award / constructive trust / JFV share]."
▶ 3C. Equalization (Married Spouses — FLA Part I) Formula
▶ NFP Formula
NFP = [Property at Valuation Date] − [Debts at VD] − [Property at DOM (except MH)] − [Excluded property s.4(2)]

Equalization Payment = (Higher NFP − Lower NFP) ÷ 2

NFP cannot be less than zero (s. 4(5))
▶ Key Dates
DateMeaningWhy It Matters
Date of Marriage (DOM)Starting pointDeduct pre-marriage assets (except MH)
Valuation Date (VD)Usually = separationAll property valued as of this date
Date of Separation (DOS)Relationship endsTriggers equalization
▶ Excluded Property — s. 4(2) FLA

(a) Property (except MH) owned at DOM → Mittler Rule: Only DOM value deducted; appreciation DOES equalize

(b) Gifts from third parties during marriage (if donor intended exclusion)

(c) Inheritances

(d) Damages for personal injuries / nervous shock

(e) Insurance proceeds (life insurance, personal injury)

(f) Property excluded by domestic contract

(g) Property traceable to any of the above

CRITICAL EXCEPTION: MATRIMONIAL HOME
MH CANNOT be excluded even if owned before marriage (s. 4(1)). If MH owned at DOM → full value at VD goes into NFP (no DOM deduction).
▶ Matrimonial Home — Special Rules (s. 17-23)

Definition: Property ordinarily occupied as family residence at DOS. Can have >1 MH.

Cannot be excluded even if owned before marriage, inherited, or gifted.

Equal right to possession (s. 19) regardless of title.

Cannot sell/mortgage without spouse's consent (s. 21).

Exclusive possession order (s. 24): Court can give one spouse exclusive right. Factors: best interest of children, financial position, other housing, violence.

▶ Unequal Division — Unconscionability s. 5(6)

Serra v Serra: Must "shock the conscience" of the court. Very high bar.

8 Factors:

(a) Failure to disclose debts/liabilities at DOM
(b) Debts recklessly incurred
(c) Part of NFP is gift from other spouse
(d) Intentional/reckless depletion of NFP
(e) NFP disproportionately large vs. cohabitation (< 5 years)
(f) Unreasonably large debts for family support
(g) Written agreement between spouses
(h) Any other circumstance relating to property

I: "The issue is the equalization of net family property."
R: Under FLA s. 5(1), the spouse with higher NFP pays half the difference. NFP = property at VD − debts − property at DOM (except MH) − excluded property (s. 4(2)). NFP ≥ 0 (s. 4(5)). MH cannot be excluded (s. 4(1)). Mittler Rule: pre-marriage assets deducted at DOM value only. Unequal division requires unconscionability (Serra: "shock the conscience") under s. 5(6).
A: [Calculate each NFP step by step; address MH; excluded property; any s. 5(6) arguments]
C: "Therefore, equalization payment = $[X]."
▶ 4A. Entitlement — Three Bases (Bracklow, 1999 SCC) Test
▶ Basis 1: Compensatory (Moge v Moge, 1992 SCC)

Marriage created economic advantage for one spouse, disadvantage for the other.

Spouse sacrificed career, education, earning capacity for family.

"Feminization of poverty" — women's income drops ~30% post-divorce.

Even childless marriages qualify if spouse curtailed career.

Rejected: "Clean break" model; strict self-sufficiency.

Key: Link between marriage roles and current disadvantage.

▶ Basis 2: Non-Compensatory / Needs-Based (Bracklow)

Economic hardship independent of marriage causation.

Illness, disability, inability to be self-sufficient.

Based on interdependence during marriage.

"No magical cut-off dates" for duration.

Key: Need exists regardless of why.

▶ Basis 3: Contractual

From express agreement (separation agreement, marriage contract, waiver).

Subject to Miglin test for enforceability.

Four Statutory Objectives (DA s. 15.2(6)):

(a) Recognize economic advantages/disadvantages from marriage or breakdown
(b) Apportion financial consequences of child care
(c) Relieve economic hardship from marriage breakdown
(d) Promote self-sufficiency within a reasonable period

Self-sufficiency is ONE objective, NOT paramount (Moge).

DA s. 15.2(5): Misconduct is NOT relevant (Leskun v Leskun, 2006 SCC).

FLA s. 29: "Spouse" for support = 3+ years cohabitation OR permanence with child.

I: "The issue is whether [party] is entitled to spousal support."
R: Under Bracklow (1999 SCC), entitlement arises on three bases: (1) compensatory — economic disadvantage from marriage roles (Moge); (2) non-compensatory — needs-based hardship; (3) contractual. DA s. 15.2(6) sets four objectives. Misconduct irrelevant (s. 15.2(5); Leskun).
A: [Which basis? What disadvantage/need? Roles during marriage?]
C: "Therefore, [party] [is/is not] entitled on [basis] grounds."
▶ 4B. Quantum — SSAG Guidelines Formula
Without Children Formula:
Amount: 1.5% to 2% of income difference × years of cohabitation
Duration: 0.5 to 1.0 years per year of cohabitation
Rule of 65: If age + years ≥ 65 → indefinite
20+ year marriages → usually indefinite
Cap: Recipient shouldn't receive > 50% of combined gross income

With Children: Different formula (lower amounts; child support priority).

Restructuring: Front-load, back-load, or lump sum (changes form, not total value).

Higher end of rangeLower end of range
Long marriage, traditional roles, significant sacrifice, health issues, older age, custodial responsibility, large income gap Short marriage, dual income, minimal sacrifice, young/healthy/employable, no children, small income gap

Variation (DA s. 17(4.1)): Requires material change in circumstances since original order (unforeseen at time of order). E.g., job loss, retirement, illness, remarriage.

I: "The issue is the quantum and duration of spousal support."
R: The SSAG provide ranges: without children, 1.5-2% of income difference × years for amount; 0.5-1.0 years per year for duration. Rule of 65 (age + years ≥ 65) = indefinite. Court considers all DA s. 15.2(6) objectives.
A: [Calculate range; identify factors pushing high/low; state specific amount]
C: "Therefore, support of $[X]/month for [duration]."

▶ 5B. Formalities — s. 55(1) FLA Statute

Must be: (1) In writing, (2) Signed by both parties, (3) Witnessed.

Missing any = agreement is VOID.
▶ 5C. Setting Aside — s. 56(4) FLA Test

(a) Failed to disclose significant assets, debts, liabilities — LeVan v LeVan (2008): deliberate non-disclosure = set aside. Leskun: "Non-disclosure is the cancer of matrimonial property litigation."

(b) Did not understand the nature or consequences — ILA is strongest evidence but not mandatory.

(c) Otherwise invalid under contract law — duress, undue influence, unconscionability, incapacity.

▶ 5D. Miglin Test (Spousal Support in Agreements) Case Law

Miglin v Miglin (2003 SCC) — Two-stage test:

Stage 1: FORMATION (How was it negotiated?)
• Vulnerability or exploitation?
• Professional assistance (lawyers)?
• Full financial disclosure?
• Pressure, urgency, duress?
• Significant power imbalance?

Stage 2: APPLICATION (Is it still fair?)
• Still substantially complies with DA objectives?
• Current circumstances reasonably anticipated?
• Material change since signing?
• Unconscionable NOW?

Result: "Significant weight" to fairly negotiated agreements UNLESS formation was unfair OR application now unconscionable.

▶ 5E. Hartshorne & Rick v Brandsema Case Law

Hartshorne v Hartshorne (2004 SCC):
• Marriage agreements get less deference than separation agreements
• Must be substantively fair at time of application
• ILA is "important" but NOT an "anodyne" (cure-all)
• Wife told agreement was "grossly unfair" → signed anyway → court enforced

Rick v Brandsema (2009 SCC):
• Unconscionability: inequality of bargaining power + improvident agreement + exploitation
• ILA is NOT a cure when party cannot meaningfully use the advice (mental condition, coercion)
• Full financial disclosure anchors "true bargain"

▶ FIRAC Template — Domestic Agreement Template
I: "The issue is whether the [agreement] is enforceable."
R: Under FLA s. 55(1), agreement must be written, signed, witnessed. Under s. 56(4), can be set aside for: (a) non-disclosure, (b) failure to understand, (c) contract law invalidity. For spousal support, Miglin two-stage: (1) formation fairness; (2) application fairness. Per Hartshorne, ILA not a cure-all. Per Rick, unconscionability can override ILA.
A: [Check formalities; s. 56(4) grounds; Miglin stages; ILA adequacy]
C: "Therefore, the agreement is [enforceable / set aside] because..."

▶ 6B. Best Interest of the Child — s. 16(3) DA Test

THE ONLY TEST. All factors weighed; no single factor is determinative.

(a) Child's needs given age & development
(b) Relationships with each parent, siblings, grandparents
(c) Each parent's willingness to support other parent's relationship ("friendly parent")
(d) History of care (status quo principle)
(e) Child's views and preferences (weight by maturity; UNCRC Art. 12)
(f) Cultural, linguistic, religious upbringing
(g) Plans for child's care
(h) Ability to communicate and cooperate
(i)-(k) Family violence (MANDATORY — see below)
(l) Any other relevant factor

▶ Family Violence — s. 16(3)(j) + s. 16(4)

When FV alleged, court MUST consider:

• Nature, seriousness, frequency of violence
• Whether child was directly or indirectly exposed
• Physical, emotional, psychological harm to child
• Whether pattern or isolated incident
• Steps taken to prevent further violence

Barendregt v Grebliunas (2022 SCC): Even ONE incident raises safety concerns. Judicial notice of IPV effects (no expert needed). Child's welfare "inextricably intertwined" with abused parent's wellbeing.

▶ Key Principles

Status quo: Courts reluctant to disrupt stable arrangements. BUT self-help (unilaterally taking children) gets less weight.

Tender years doctrine: REJECTED. No presumption young children belong with mother. Gender-neutral.

Maximum contact (s. 16(6)): Child should have as much time with each parent as consistent with BIC. NOT absolute — safety trumps contact (Barendregt).

Van de Perre v Edwards (2001 SCC): Race is relevant but NOT determinative.

I: "The issue is the parenting arrangement in the best interest of the child."
R: Under DA s. 16(1), the sole consideration is best interest, per s. 16(3) factors: needs, relationships, willingness to support other parent, history of care, child's views, FV (mandatory — s. 16(3)(j)). Barendregt (2022 SCC): even one FV incident raises safety concerns. Maximum contact (s. 16(6)) qualified by safety. Status quo carries weight but not if by self-help.
A: [Walk through each relevant factor; address FV if present; weigh factors]
C: "Therefore, [sole/joint] decision-making with [schedule] serves the child's best interest."
▶ 6C. Relocation — Barendregt (2022 SCC) Test

Notice: 60 days written notice (DA s. 16.9)

Burden of proof (s. 16.93):

• Relocating parent has majority time → OTHER parent must show NOT in BIC
• Equal/near-equal time → RELOCATING parent must show IS in BIC
• Minority time → relocating parent bears burden

Factors (s. 16.92): Reasons for move, impact on child, existing arrangements, whether notice given, reasonable access plan.

FV is relevant: Victim relocating for safety = strong justification. Improving abused parent's condition = enriches child's environment.

I: "The issue is whether [parent] should be permitted to relocate."
R: Under DA s. 16.9, 60 days notice required. Burden depends on existing time (s. 16.93). Factors: reasons, impact, existing arrangements, FV (Barendregt). Child's welfare tied to nurturing parent's wellbeing.
A: [Who has majority time? Reason for move? Impact on access? FV? Access plan?]
C: "Therefore, relocation [should/should not] be permitted."
▶ 7A. "Child of the Marriage" — DA s. 2(1)

Under age of majority AND has not withdrawn from parental charge
OR over age of majority but UNABLE to withdraw due to: illness, disability, or other cause (e.g., full-time post-secondary)

Includes: biological, adopted, step-children treated as children of the marriage.

▶ 7B. Table Amount (Baseline) Formula
Payor's annual gross income → Cross-reference with # of children → Ontario table → Monthly amount

This is the presumptive amount. Departure requires justification.
▶ 7C. Adjustments Test
▶ Shared Custody — s. 9 (40% Rule)

If EACH parent has child ≥ 40% of the time:

• Consider both parents' table amounts
• Consider increased costs of shared custody
• Consider child's needs & means of each parent
• NOT simply offsetting table amounts

Major litigation driver — financial incentive to reach 40% threshold.
▶ Split Custody — s. 8

Each parent has custody of one or more children. Each pays table amount for children with other parent. Set off — lower pays difference.

▶ High Income — s. 4 (>$150K)

Table applies to first $150K. Above that → court has discretion (can apply table % to excess or determine based on child's needs).

▶ Undue Hardship — s. 10 (Very High Bar)

Must show: (a) Hardship from guidelines amount (high marriage debts, high access costs, duty to support another person/child) AND (b) Household standard of living LOWER than other household.

Even if proven → court MAY (not must) depart.

▶ 7D. Special/Extraordinary Expenses — s. 7 Formula

Types: Childcare, health costs, extraordinary extracurriculars, post-secondary, private school.

Formula: Each parent pays PROPORTIONAL to their share of combined income.
E.g., Parent A earns 60% → pays 60% of expense. NOT 50/50 unless equal incomes.

Must be "necessary" and "reasonable."

▶ 7E. Imputing Income Case Law

Court can impute income when parent is: intentionally unemployed/underemployed, diverting income (to corporation/trust), property not generating income, failed to provide info, unreasonable deductions.

Colucci v Colucci (2021 SCC): Framework for income imputation.

▶ FIRAC Template — Child Support Template

Adjustments: shared custody (s. 9, 40%), split custody (s. 8), high income (s. 4, >$150K), undue hardship (s. 10). Special expenses (s. 7) shared proportionally. Income may be imputed (Colucci, 2021 SCC).
